HISTORY

Wealth isn't just saved; it's inherited.

To understand your money today, we have to look at the systems that built yesterday. From redlining to lending bias, the path hasn't been equal.

1. The Legacy of Redlining

In the 1930s, the government created maps that color-coded neighborhoods. "Red" areas—mostly Black communities—were deemed "hazardous" for investment, denying millions the ability to build home equity for decades.

"Home equity is the single largest driver of middle-class wealth in America. Redlining effectively locked a generation out of the American Dream."

2. The GI Bill Exclusion

While the GI Bill built the white middle class after WWII, Black veterans were systematically denied the same low-interest mortgages and education benefits, widening the racial wealth gap at a critical economic moment.

3. Modern Challenges: The "Black Tax"

Appraisal Bias

Studies show homes in Black neighborhoods are often undervalued by appraisers compared to identical homes in white areas.

Lending Disparities

Borrowers of color often face higher interest rates even when credit scores are comparable, leading to higher lifetime costs for debt.

4. Reclaiming the Narrative

Understanding these hurdles isn't about defeat—it's about strategy. Building generational wealth today requires intentionality, community support, and financial literacy.

Empowerment Tip

Support Community Development Financial Institutions (CDFIs) which are designed to invest in underserved markets.
